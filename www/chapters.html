KEY DEFINITIONS

One of the most important key elements during an UX process is being able to define all aspects surrounding your product, whether it being the target audience, a customers journey through your product or even the concept itself.

Step 1: Personas

Personas are fictitious, specific, concrete representations of target users based on user research which will help you become more user focused and are an essential part of goal-directed design.

Creating personas for your project involves morphing qualitative and quantitative data from analyctics, surveys, interviews, user testing sessions, and other research activities into a handful of representative “typical” users/archetypes.

Important questions

1. Who are they?

2. What are their values?

3. What is their demography?

4. What needs do they have?

5. What are their goals?

A scenario is a narrative describing “a day in the life of” one of your personas, and probably includes how your website or app fits into their lives.

Step 4: Define The Content

All the research is in place, you now define all the content your product will contain. If you’re going to have a text block with information, define what this block says in black and white. If you only have visuals, write down what they are visualizing, as it helps mapping out the flow of your communication.

Notes

1. Product content should reflect project goals and user’s needs

2. It should go hand-in-hand with the design of the product

3. It should be consistent, useful and accessible for users.


Keep in mind that some content will probably be changed during the process, and in some cases even removed.

DESIGN

You’ve defined your product to its core, and its time to visualize these definitions.
UX designers have a unique set of skills that help bridge the mental gap between words and images.
Be careful not to go into specific visual details too quickly, as you risk losing focus of the big picture.

Step 1: Sitemap

A sitemap is a complete list of all pages/content available on a website or in an app.

Creating a sitemap of your content is a useful task at the beginning of the design process, as it can be used to shape which screens to wireframe.

Step 5: Sketching

Sketching is very similar to brainstorming an idea, and is about experimenting and exploring to arrive at a good design solution communicating both the content and the concept of your project.

Avoid details, and note that this is neither a wireframe or a prototype.

Put a lot of work into this process until you feel confident to move on with the wireframes.

Step 6: Wireframes

A wireframe is a rough guide for the layout of a website or app. A prototype is similar in that while far from being a polished product in therms of visuals or functionality, it gives an indication of the direction that the product is heading.

Static wireframes can be created with pen and paper, but these days the software available for creating interactive wireframes makes the task quick and easy to do.

Wireframes can be done in multiple desktop softwares, but also with in-browser editors like uxpin.

Step 7: Low-Fidelity Prototyping

Creating a low fidelity prototype (on paper) is a must before you start doing any digital work beyond the wireframe.

It’s fast, efficient and help us to validate important product concepts early in the design process.

Step 8: Low-Fidelity User Testing

Before you put users in front of your prototype, write down certain functions of your product you want to test.

When users test your product ask them to perform these certain tasks, and to think out loud while doing so.

Tip

Step 9: Unmoderated Remote Usability Test

URUT is similar to in-person usability testing, however, participants complete tasks in their own environment without you moderating the process.

The tasks are pre-determined and are presented to the participant via an online testing/prototype platform in the likes of InVision, Marvel, Proto, or POP.

Step 10: Review Test Results

Look at the results of your Low-Fidelity User Tests, what worked, what didn’t?

If you have any new ideas or thoughts based on the results, put together new wireframes and run them through the next step, the A/B Testing Phase.

Step 11: A/B Testing

A/B testing is a method of comparing two versions of a website or an app against each other to determine which one performs better.

It’s a good idea to get this done in this low-fidelity design phase to make sure you don’t end up completing the design with functions which doesn't work.

How does it work?

Give one version of a page or feature to some users; give another version to the rest, and measure the performance of each to see which was more successful.

PRODUCTION

The Production Phase is where the high-fidelity design is fleshed out, content and digital assets are created, and a high-fidelity version of the product is validated with stakeholders and end-users through user testing sessions.

Step 1: UI Elements

How does your icons, buttons, fonts like? What colour scheme are you going to use to communicate your product? You might have an idea, but by creating a simple guideline for your visual style you’ll make the production phase a bit easier.

Look at styleguides.io for some inspiration.

Step 2: Design The Product

So, your LFP user-testing was a success and the UI elements are in place? Put down the pen and paper and start digitalizing the wireframe using software in the likes of Sketch, Affinity or Photoshop.

When you’re done designing the layout for the main pages, it’s time to run them through a second step of moderated and unmoderated user testing sequences.

Step 3: High-Fidelity Prototyping

A high-fidelity prototype is essentially the same as a low-fidelity prototype, but based on an almost complete digital design, using ui-elements, icons, colors and pictures.

The reason I say almost, is because by doing the high-fidelity test you’ll be able to see if the users respond the same to your interface as they did before. Are you able to lead your users through your website or app with the current color scheme, or iconography? Do they click what they’re supposed to?

Step 4: High-Fidelity User Testing

Before you put users in front of your prototype, write down certain functions of your product you want to test. See if the users respond to these functions without you telling them, but make sure you tell them to think out loud while testing the prototype.

It’s always a good idea to document the users testing your design, by either taking notes, filming or both.

Step 6: Review Test Results

Look at the results of your High-Fidelity User Tests, what worked, what didn’t? Make the changes needed and run a new user testing phase.

Step 7: Finalised Style Guide

A style guide establishes a common UI language for your website or app and help with providing a consistent visual experience across platforms.

What to do:

Gather all elements like; icons, typography and color scheme from your interface. Place them in a single document and label them.

For a front end style guide you should define all the base styles like; h-elements, links, lists etc.

Include interaction states like; hover, visited states on links and buttons

Make the document easy to read and easy to maintain

BETA LAUNCH

A beta release usually means that a website or application has had all of the major known issues fixed but has not been tested enough for an official release. The main purpose of a beta release is to provide a fully functioning demo of a website or application.

Step 1: Closed Beta Launch

Releasing a closed beta release of your product involves allowing only a select group of users to use the software and provide feedback before it becomes available to the wider public.

In addition to feedback, you’ll be able to fix flaws, bugs and crashes, making sure the final launch is stable.

Learn more

Top Twelve Tips For Running A Beta Test

Step 2: Review Beta Launch

Review beta launch results, fix bugs, crashes and optimize all aspects of your products before even thinking about doing a final launch.

Step 3: Final Launch

Everything is in order, and working as intended. The product does not miss any key elements and is now ready to be released to the public!
